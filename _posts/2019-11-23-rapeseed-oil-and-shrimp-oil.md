昨天肚子饿翻冰箱，发现仍然有一小盒冻虾，想起来是半个多月前囤货的时候买的，因为之前买到了一斤鲜虾先吃活的了，没有处理，日子久也就忘了。第二天早饭自然是有了着落，我取出虾盒，放去泡水解冻，然后打开灶台边的虾油罐子——已经见底了，于是今天又用剥下的虾壳熬了些。不得不夸虾油炒菜的香味，锅刚温热，冷油下锅，不用花椒蒜末炝锅，便激起一股浓厚的香气，就像喝热牛奶时扑到鼻孔的温热浓郁的气味，但要更加的鲜。这种香料油用葵花籽油熬制是最好的，豆油次之，因为味道淡，不会冲了虾的香气，但家里一桶豆油已经见了底，手边就只剩一香油瓶子，用麻油做自然是不可能。余光撇到角落一小桶漆黑的菜籽油，这才放心地去剥虾。

菜籽油有着很特别的气味，是很冲鼻的青涩气味。像是浓茶漱口时涌上鼻腔的感觉，让人不由得缩一缩鼻子。我对这种味道很熟悉了，但谈不上喜爱或者是厌恶，只是每次吃到菜油炒的菜，会下意识地反应一下——啊，这是菜油炒的，只此而已。乡里老家，爷爷奶奶有一片田，他们闲来无聊种一些，另外一片春天就包给别人种些油菜。春天能吸引一些蝴蝶，等到过几个月，开了花，就是一片金灿灿的花池子。并不能说是花海，因为那块田在溱湖边上，隔着就是一望无际的大湖，称作花海未免夸张了。也正因紧邻大湖，土壤有鱼虾的润泽，油菜也格外茂盛，很小的时候，我甚至没有菜杆高，往往在田间玩耍，跑进油菜丛中，别人就看不见了。常用这招躲过家人，好在外面多闹一会，或者当作隐蔽物，躲在里面，趁爷爷奶奶不注意，偷偷跑下河口。

"河口有水鬼！小心把你拖下去！"他们常常这么说。

等到花落下了，就成了一丛一丛的细长豆荚，青绿色的，竹叶卷起来与之类似，但是豆荚里包的是小米粒样的种子，青绿的时候未能收获，因为水分很多，也没多少油脂，但剥开豆荚，用手小心地碾碎一两粒，却是一股带着淡淡的清爽气味。这种味道还是很容易分辨的，不同于菜油带着油脂的温润和植物汁液挥发性的猛烈，在未成熟时，它被水分淡化成了轻松的植物香气，让人联想到清晨带着露珠的草地，刚刚修剪过树枝桠的公园，随手泡的一杯淡茶和偷偷摘下的樟树叶，但终究有油脂味道，因此比起那些更加平和。每到这时我总会感谢坚强的鼻子，虽然鼻炎，但并没有让我失去对于气味的敏感，只是偶尔刺鼻的味道和冷风多打几个喷嚏。

过了盛夏，油菜走进了它自己的秋季。到了终末的收获，油菜只剩下灰黄的枯叶颜色。原先粗壮的茎干变成了小拇指粗细的柴草条，青绿饱满的豆荚也逐渐由墨绿的干瘦转为干瘪，未收时，田地依旧是一片金色，但变得瘦小稀疏。西风吹过时，能听到悦耳清脆的沙沙声，不同于树叶摩挲的低沉奏鸣，它更加稀疏明亮，传播不了太远。当你站在田边时，会听见的，这是干枯的叶，茎和菜荚破碎崩裂的声音，是菜籽撕裂包衣，茎秆撞击折断，是油菜选择的最后的壮丽，生的终末的哀乐，它以清脆欢快的方式表达。

最后成熟的菜籽被从豆荚中脱出来，炒熟压制出甘美的油脂。灯光下，它是纯粹的金色，如刚从颜料管中挤出的黄色浓缩染料，透着一股化不开的倔强，因此炒出来的菜总金灿灿的，盘底黄澄澄一片，总觉得没吃干净。气味也一如既往的冲，因此熬成的虾油仍然带着这样的独特味道。纯粹的金，融进虾的鲜红，作出明亮的橙红色。熬完油的虾壳也变得金灿灿的了，滤过油后我尝了一个，完全脱去水分的酥脆的口感加上虾油的肉类鲜美，颇有干嚼猪油渣的快乐。我本打算回锅加热到冒烟，去除菜油味，但这味道慢慢淡化在口中，解了肉类的腥腻和烟火气，于是欣然作罢。

先前的一罐虾油被我用来下了阳春面，一连吃了两周。这罐不多，不过有点炸过了头，用来凉拌倒是浓香。听说最近河虾便宜起来，突然喜欢上虾了，有空复原一次三虾面吧。

Yesterday, hungry, I rummaged through the fridge and found there was still a small box of frozen shrimp — I recalled buying it more than half a month ago when stocking up, since I'd gotten a jin of fresh shrimp before and eaten the live ones first, leaving these unprocessed and, in time, forgotten. So breakfast the next morning was taken care of. I took out the box of shrimp, set it to soak and thaw, and then opened the jar of shrimp oil by the stove — already scraped to the bottom — so today I rendered some more from the peeled shells. I can't help but praise the aroma of stir-frying in shrimp oil: the pan just warm, the cold oil going in, and without so much as Sichuan pepper or minced garlic to start it off, a thick fragrance rises up — like the warm, rich smell that hits your nose when you drink hot milk, only fresher. This flavored oil is best rendered in sunflower-seed oil, soybean oil second, because their taste is light and won't overpower the shrimp's fragrance; but the tin of soybean oil at home was scraped bare, and all that was left at hand was a bottle of sesame oil — and using sesame oil, of course, was out of the question. Out of the corner of my eye I caught a small tin of pitch-black rapeseed oil in the corner, and only then set to peeling the shrimp with an easy mind.

Rapeseed oil has a very particular smell — a sharp, green, raw pungency. It's like the sensation of strong tea surging up into your nose when you rinse your mouth with it, making you can't help but scrunch up your nose. I know this smell well, though I can't say I love it or loathe it; it's just that every time I eat a dish stir-fried in rapeseed oil, I react without thinking — ah, this was done in rapeseed oil — and nothing more. Back in the countryside my grandparents had a plot of land, part of which they planted for something to do in their idleness, while the other part they'd rent out in spring for someone to grow rapeseed. In spring it drew some butterflies, and a few months on, once it bloomed, it became a pond of golden flowers. You couldn't quite call it a sea of flowers, because the plot sat by Qin Lake, and just beyond stretched the boundless lake — "sea of flowers" would be an exaggeration. And it was precisely because it bordered the great lake, the soil nourished by fish and shrimp, that the rapeseed grew especially lush. When I was very small, shorter even than the stalks, I'd often play in the field, run into the thick of the rapeseed, and no one could see me. I often used this trick to dodge my family and stay out romping a while longer, or as cover to hide inside, and then, while my grandparents weren't looking, sneak down to the river mouth.

"There are water ghosts at the river mouth! Watch out or they'll drag you under!" they often said.

Once the flowers fell, they became clusters of slender green pods — a curled bamboo leaf looks much like them — but wrapped inside the pods were seeds like grains of millet. Green, they couldn't yet be harvested, holding too much water and little oil; but split a pod open and carefully crush a seed or two between your fingers, and there's a faint, fresh scent. This smell is easy enough to tell apart: unlike the oily warmth of rapeseed oil and the volatile fierceness of plant sap, in its unripe state the water dilutes it into an easy, green fragrance — bringing to mind a dew-covered lawn at dawn, a park where the branches have just been pruned, a cup of weak tea steeped on a whim, and a camphor leaf picked on the sly — yet in the end there's still a hint of oil to it, so it's gentler than any of those. At times like these I'm always grateful for my sturdy nose; though I have rhinitis, it hasn't cost me my sensitivity to smell — only, now and then, a few extra sneezes at a sharp odor or a cold wind.

Past midsummer, the rapeseed entered an autumn of its own. By the final harvest, it was left only the grey-yellow color of withered leaves. The once-sturdy stalks had become firewood-thin twigs no thicker than a little finger, and the plump green pods had gone gradually from a lean dark green to shriveled. Before the reaping, the field was still a sheet of gold, but thin and sparse now. When the west wind blew across it, you could hear a pleasant, crisp rustling — unlike the low murmur of leaves brushing together, it was sparser and brighter, and didn't carry far. Stand at the edge of the field and you'll hear it: the sound of dry leaves, stalks, and pods breaking and splitting apart, of seeds tearing free of their husks and stems knocking and snapping — the last splendor that the rapeseed chooses, a funeral music at the end of life, expressed in a crisp and joyful way.

The last of the ripe seeds are shaken free of their pods, roasted, and pressed into a sweet, rich oil. Under lamplight it is pure gold, like concentrated yellow dye just squeezed from a paint tube, with an obstinacy that won't dissolve — so dishes fried in it always come out golden, a wash of bright yellow at the bottom of the plate that always leaves you feeling you haven't eaten it clean. Its smell is as pungent as ever, and so the shrimp oil rendered with it still carries this distinctive note. Pure gold melts into the shrimp's bright red to make a bright orange-red. The shrimp shells, once the oil is rendered, turn golden too; after straining out the oil I tasted one — the crisp texture of something wholly dried out, plus the meaty savor of shrimp oil, had much of the pleasure of chewing dry pork cracklings. I'd meant to return it to the pan and heat it until it smoked, to drive off the rapeseed-oil taste, but the flavor slowly faded in my mouth, cutting the fishy richness of the meat and the smoke of the fire, so I happily let it be.

The previous jar of shrimp oil I used for plain yangchun noodles, eating them two weeks running. This jar isn't much, and it was fried a touch too long, but it's richly fragrant for dressing cold dishes. I hear river shrimp have gotten cheap lately; I've suddenly taken a liking to shrimp — when I have time, I'll recreate a bowl of "three-shrimp" noodles.
