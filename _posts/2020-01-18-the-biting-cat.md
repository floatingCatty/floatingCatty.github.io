今天脑袋上灰灰的那只猫又给我妈的手上添了点红。起因是她向我诉苦说，这只猫总不让抱，每次都不安分地跳下来，我不信，因为最近几天，我写东西或者看书的时候，这只猫总会蹭到我身边，蹦上腿。母亲当然不愿承认照顾了几个月的猫被我刚回来几天就拐跑了，于是乘猫不注意，又抱了起来。果然，猫睁大了棕绿的眼睛盯着我妈，两只脚小心试探地蹬了几下，接着转身蛟猫入海一样地跳了下来。然而两只爪子仍然勾着，给我妈手上留下了两道血印。

家人形容这只猫，总会用到贼眉鼠眼这个词。的确，和另一只相比，这只猫不但因短毛而显得光溜溜的，眼睛也是近乎一个完整的圆，仿佛不存在上下眼皮一样，总是保持着这个样子到处张望。回家了，他第一个冲出来，远远地看着你，谁出了房间，打开门，他便乘机钻进去，要是顽皮被发现了，仍旧微微低着头，一脸委屈地用它的圆圆的眼睛瞅着你，而且往往是在刚刚抓或者咬疼你的时候，让人既生气又好笑。另一只则贵气许多，因为是波斯和狸花的杂交，样子就偏向波斯猫，纯白长毛，异色瞳，宽脸，平时也总是温雅的神态，然而吃饭的时候却会露出凶相，霸占着食物，发出嗤嗤的声音不让咬人猫接近，因为家里取得很多名字叫它俩一直都不答应，于是猫的名字至今也没确定，于是今天写的这只我就暂且叫它咬人猫了。

据说咬人猫刚到家时便咬人，当时它十分胆小，见到人便往床下面钻，又经常被另一只欺负，于是到哪里都小心翼翼地，走路好似垫着脚，一步一步地试探着迈腿。等到我放假回来的时候，虽然和它已经和人熟络，但是谨慎的样子仍然保持着，也许这就是"贼气"的来源。

刚开始我甚至有些怕他，每次和他逗乐，手刚刚碰到毛的末梢，咬人猫便触电一样转过身来，用爪子和嘴同时够你的手，反应不灵敏的，便会落下几道或白或红的牙印。若是此时心理还存着逗猫的念头，就会变成一场砸地鼠的游戏：咬人猫将圆眼睛的视线全部集中在你还没伸出的手上，但你一有动作，小白爪子便迫不及待地砸上去，如果你自信可以及时缩手，这便会成为一场持久的战斗，当然，最终挂彩的额肯定是你。因此在刚开始，我并不敢主动逗他，每次都是假装看风景，等他好奇我的动作，走到我身边，这才试着和他接触。最初几次，我只敢微微碰碰他的脑袋，揉揉那挫山字形的黑毛，渐渐躲避他爪子的功夫熟练了，才能摸到他的背，他自然不会老实让你撸，手刚从脖子滑到背部上方，小脑袋就转了过来，往往是突然一口，咬住不松开。

<div class="post-en" markdown="1">

Today the cat with the grey patch on its head added a little red to my mother's hand again. It started when she complained to me that this cat never lets itself be held — every time, it restlessly jumps down. I didn't believe her, because over the last few days, whenever I was writing or reading, the cat would rub up against me and hop onto my lap. My mother, naturally unwilling to admit that the cat she'd looked after for months had been lured away by me within days of my coming home, waited until it wasn't paying attention and picked it up again. Sure enough, the cat fixed its wide brown-green eyes on her, gave a few cautious, testing kicks with its feet, then twisted around and leapt down like a dragon diving into the sea. But its two paws were still hooked in, and they left two bloody scratches on her hand.

When my family describes this cat, they always reach for the phrase "shifty-eyed." It's true — compared with the other one, this cat is not only sleek and smooth from its short fur, but its eyes are almost perfect circles, as if it had no eyelids at all, always kept that way as it looks about. When I come home, he's the first to rush out, watching you from a distance; the moment someone leaves a room and opens the door, he seizes the chance to slip in, and if he's caught being naughty he still lowers his head slightly and peers at you with those round eyes, full of grievance — usually right after he's just scratched or bitten you, which leaves you both angry and amused. The other one is far more aristocratic: a cross between a Persian and a tabby, it takes after the Persian — pure white long fur, mismatched eyes, a broad face, and usually a gentle, refined air; yet at mealtimes it bares a fierce look, monopolizing the food and hissing to keep the biting cat away. We've given the two of them many names, and they've never once answered to any, so to this day their names remain unsettled — and so, for now, I'll just call the one I'm writing about today the biting cat.

They say the biting cat bit people the moment it arrived. Back then it was terribly timid — at the sight of a person it would burrow under the bed, and it was often bullied by the other cat, so it went everywhere with great caution, walking as if on tiptoe, testing each step before setting the leg down. By the time I came home for the holiday, it had grown familiar with people, but that wary manner remained — and perhaps that is the source of its "shiftiness."

At first I was even a little afraid of him. Every time I played with him, the instant my hand touched the tips of his fur the biting cat would spin around as if electrified, reaching for your hand with claw and mouth at once; if your reflexes were slow, you'd be left with a few tooth-marks, white or red. And if at that point you still had a mind to tease the cat, it turned into a game of whack-a-mole: the biting cat would fix the whole line of sight of its round eyes on the hand you hadn't yet extended, but the moment you moved, its little white paws would smack down, unable to wait; and if you were confident you could pull back in time, it became a drawn-out battle — in which, of course, the one who ends up wounded is always you. So at the beginning I didn't dare tease him first; each time I'd pretend to admire the view, and only once he grew curious about my movements and came over to me would I try to make contact. The first few times, I only dared touch his head lightly and rub the black fur on it, shaped like a little three-peaked mountain; as I gradually got better at dodging his paws, I could reach his back. He would of course never sit still to be stroked — the moment my hand slid from his neck to the top of his back, his little head would turn, and often, all at once, he'd clamp down and not let go.
