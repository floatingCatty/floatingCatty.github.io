回家的时候闻到一种熟悉的味道，直接的说是点燃的香的味道，而且是廉价线香带着烟火气，微微刺鼻的香味，好像从气味中就能想象出样貌：土黄色松松散散的粗糙木屑，混合了各种奇怪的粉状香味添加物，用化学胶水勉强粘合在一起，没有什么特别的。可能是在外面久了，很久没有闻，才突然注意到。可能对我来说这也算是家的味道之一，但家里点的是稍稍细腻一些的黑色线香，看起来光滑的多，味道却一样，大概也是很廉价的了，不过即使到寺院，最常闻到的还是这样的化学气息，据说佛祖菩萨饮食的就是香燃烧时的烟气，大约那些大士佛陀们与我等人间小民一样，平日也只是用最便宜的饭菜充饥了。

虽然从小一直闻着香长大，但现在叫我说起来，却认识不了几种，除了按形态能说出线香、盘香、斗香，其他的就一概不知了，不另说什么名贵的品种，诸如龙延香，沉香什么的，只是耳闻，并不能想象。我也不能因为写文就强行扯出自己与香的缘分，这样读者不但一看就不信这样种种珍奇的事情，我自己写起瞎话也是全无益处，只好老老实实说些无聊的记忆，勉强凑出文章来。

家里逢月初十五会点香，通常点在厨房外墙柜子中的观音瓷像和阳台地上位于花草中的小铜香坛里，而这两处地方一南一北串通起客厅和餐厅，因为中间没有隔断，每到点香的日子，穿堂风就把两边的香气持续地吹散到整个家里。与其说是被吹散，我更觉得香自己会走动，有倔强灵魂似的，即使我关着屋门，它仍然要挤过门缝让我的鼻子痒痒一阵。虽说点香，但我父母并不信佛，依照模糊的印象，是奶奶在我很小的时候要求的，有时候突然到月半来突击检查，发现没有点便和父亲唠叨好一阵，后来还了一大把香来，父亲刚开始总埋怨着这些麻烦事儿，日子久了，这倒成了家里一个必不可少的仪式了，有时候他出门在外，总是要叮嘱家里人，于是我们就随意的点几根。若是他自己，则还要不辞辛劳的精心挑上三根同样长短的线香，点燃后双手持香成山字形，朝着铜香炉和菩萨像拜上三拜，然后恭恭敬敬地插在香灰里。这些年家里的点的香从来没有断过，但是我们并没有添置新香，仍然用从前奶奶送的那把，因为记不清当时她送来多少了，想象中总会出现奶奶拖着一个装满香的大蛇皮袋敲门的情形，以前真让我害怕过好一阵子，现在想起来便不停憋笑。

奶奶信佛，这是她自己介绍和传教时总说的话，但是具体信的除了各处的菩萨佛祖，还有各种道教的神仙，每次聊天她总会不厌其烦地劝我信佛好，信佛好，有一次我倒厌烦了，于是开始用各种佛教道教地基础知识考她，比如太乙真人原名观音大士性别之类的，奶奶一下子没有话讲了，我原本以为自己会感到很得意且轻松，但破坏别人心意和信仰终归是邪恶的罪行，我突然开始内疚起来，于是以后总很热心地听她讲神仙故事。奶奶家的香自然是不会断绝的，除了线香，总点的还有盘香，放在灯心草做的油灯旁边，所有这些都放在客厅进门朝着的黑漆木条桌上，除了香具，长明灯，还有香烛，红纸之类的祭祀用品，像极了寺庙菩萨前供人跪拜的小方桌，只是桌子最右边摆放着微波炉这个极为不相称的东西，虽然破坏了神秘的气氛，倒也透出一股朴素农村生活的气息了，大概对奶奶来说，跪拜烧香是和吃饭睡觉一样平凡的事情，只是我们没"开窍"还没有明白罢了。

每天早晨 4 点，奶奶都会准时起床，填灰，添油，跪拜，是她每日必修的早课，假期我住在那里的时候，早晨总是被袅袅香气唤醒，因为那个屋子是青砖垒砌的，房间顶上有两块砖头大小的天窗，如果碰到阳光好的时候，斜射进来的光柱中可以看到慢慢悠悠飘荡着的烟，像轻轻浮动的绸缎带子，也不像普通的烟雾，总也见不到其散开消失。小时候每次回去住总呆不久，一面是因为老家的我住的屋子离天井太近，每到晚上野猫耗子和各种昆虫总发出瘆人的声音，让我怎么也睡不好，另一面也是因为受不了稻草木板床的硌。现在却常常想起那里来，老家的石头屋子建在河边，四周有几户人家，更多的是田和树，出门走到河边的小渡口能看见远处的稀疏村落，没有电线和高楼，除了极少数的路灯，并没有很多人工的气息。生活的味道倒是很浓，门外脚下用碎瓦铺成的小路旁摆着拉东西用的三轮，隔壁废弃了的二楼的柱子也让爬上丝瓜藤，离河边仅有的几米土地也被用起来长了青菜草莓，另一片因为有坡度，无法种菜，也被植上了一颗银杏树，一颗桑树，桑树至今不知道是用来做什么，但银杏每年都能收获不少白果。

上午有意续写这篇的时候，我自己也取了一根线香在房间点了，手捏着一头，像玩仙女棒一样挥来挥去，本来脑补出用青烟当画笔，在空中画出不同图案来，然后可以美美地写上一大段，结果无论我怎么摇晃，烟气总是一个样：直直地从香头冒出来，然后左右摇摆出波浪地样子，接着变成丝带，不停折叠、翻转，分裂成无数缕更细碎的浅色绸带，最终越分越小而消失不见，有时让人怀疑烟并未消失，而是因为人的眼力所限，不能捕捉罢了。偶尔会有一些小绸带重新组合起来，飘到更远一点的地方，心里就一直期望能有几缕青丝化为漂流变化的云，长久地停留在屋子里，于是我极力让手静止不动，胳膊虽然麻痹了，结果并没有太大变化，也都是分解消散了。

Coming home, I caught a familiar smell — put plainly, the smell of burning incense, and cheap stick incense at that, with a whiff of smoke and a faintly sharp scent, as if from the smell alone you could picture it: loose, coarse, earth-yellow sawdust mixed with all sorts of strange powdered fragrance additives, barely held together with chemical glue, nothing special. Perhaps it was because I'd been away so long and hadn't smelled it in ages that I suddenly noticed it. Perhaps for me this, too, counts as one of the smells of home — though what we burn at home is a slightly finer black stick incense, much smoother-looking but the same in smell, and probably just as cheap. Yet even at a temple, what you most often smell is this same chemical scent. They say the Buddhas and bodhisattvas dine on the smoke of burning incense; I suppose those great saints and buddhas, like us lowly mortals, usually make do with the cheapest fare to fill their bellies.

Though I grew up breathing incense from childhood, if you asked me now I couldn't name many kinds — apart from telling them apart by shape as stick, coil, or "dou" incense, I know none of the rest, let alone the precious varieties like ambergris or agarwood, which I've only heard of and cannot picture. Nor can I, for the sake of writing, force out some deep affinity between me and incense; a reader would see through such rare and wondrous claims at a glance, and there'd be no use in my spinning lies either — so I can only honestly relate some dull memories and scrape a piece together.

At home we burn incense on the first and fifteenth of the lunar month, usually before the porcelain Guanyin in the cupboard on the kitchen's outer wall and in the little bronze censer set among the flowers and plants on the balcony floor. These two spots, one south and one north, link the living room and dining room; because there's no partition between them, on incense days the draft through the house keeps carrying the scent from both sides throughout the home. Rather than being carried, though, I feel the incense moves of its own accord, as if it had a stubborn soul: even with my door shut, it still insists on squeezing through the crack to set my nose itching a while. We burn incense, but my parents aren't Buddhist; by my vague impression, it was my grandmother who required it when I was very small. Sometimes she'd show up unannounced at mid-month for a surprise inspection, and finding none lit she'd nag my father for a good while, and later brought over a great bundle of incense. At first my father always grumbled about the bother of it, but with time it became an indispensable ritual of the household; when he's away, he always reminds the family, and so we casually light a few sticks. If he does it himself, though, he goes to the trouble of carefully picking three sticks of the same length, holds them in both hands in the shape of the character 山 after lighting them, bows three times toward the bronze censer and the bodhisattva image, and then reverently plants them in the incense ash. Over the years the incense at home has never once run out, yet we've never bought any new — we still use that bundle my grandmother gave us long ago. Since I can't remember how much she brought at the time, my imagination always conjures the scene of her knocking at the door dragging a big woven sack stuffed with incense; it genuinely frightened me for quite a while back then, and now the memory makes me stifle a laugh again and again.

My grandmother is a Buddhist — that's what she always says when introducing herself and preaching — but what she actually believes in, besides the bodhisattvas and buddhas of every kind, includes all sorts of Taoist immortals. Every time we chatted she'd tirelessly urge me that believing in the Buddha is good, believing is good; once I got fed up and began quizzing her on basic Buddhist and Taoist knowledge — the original name of Taiyi Zhenren, the gender of Guanyin, and the like — and my grandmother was suddenly at a loss for words. I'd thought I would feel smug and relieved, but destroying another's heart and faith is, after all, an evil sin, and I was suddenly stricken with guilt, so ever after I always listened eagerly to her tales of the immortals. The incense at my grandmother's house, of course, never runs out: besides stick incense, there's always coil incense burning, set beside an oil lamp made with a rush wick. All of it sits on the black-lacquered narrow wooden table that faces you as you enter the living room — besides the incense implements and the ever-burning lamp, there are also candles, red paper, and other offerings, very like the little square table before the bodhisattvas in a temple where people kneel and bow, except that at the far right of the table sits a microwave, that utterly incongruous thing, which, though it breaks the air of mystery, lets a plain rural life show through. Perhaps for my grandmother, kneeling and burning incense are as ordinary as eating and sleeping, and it's only we who haven't been "enlightened" yet who don't understand.

Every morning at four o'clock, my grandmother rises on time to refill the ash, add oil, and kneel in prayer — her daily morning devotions. When I stayed there over the holidays, I was always woken in the morning by the curling fragrance. The house is built of grey brick, with two brick-sized skylights in the ceiling, and when the sun was good you could see, in the slanting shafts of light, the smoke drifting slowly and leisurely, like softly floating ribbons of silk — unlike ordinary smoke, you never saw it spread and disappear. As a child I never stayed long on these visits, partly because the room I slept in at the old house was too close to the courtyard, where every night wild cats, mice, and all manner of insects made eerie sounds that kept me from sleeping well, and partly because I couldn't stand the poke of the straw-and-plank bed. Now, though, I often think of the place. The old stone house was built by the river, with a few households around it and, more than that, fields and trees; step out to the little ferry landing by the river and you could see sparse villages in the distance — no power lines or tall buildings, and, apart from a very few streetlights, not much sign of the man-made. The flavor of life, on the other hand, was thick: beside the little path of broken tiles underfoot outside the door stood a tricycle for hauling things; a luffa vine had been let climb the pillars of the abandoned second floor next door; even the few meters of land by the river had been put to use growing greens and strawberries, and another patch, too sloped to plant vegetables, had been set with a ginkgo tree and a mulberry — to this day I don't know what the mulberry is for, but the ginkgo yields a good many white nuts every year.

This morning, meaning to go on with this piece, I lit a stick of incense in the room myself, pinched one end, and waved it around like playing with a sparkler. I'd imagined using the blue smoke as a brush to draw different patterns in the air, and then writing a fine long passage about it — but no matter how I shook it, the smoke was always the same: rising straight up from the tip, then swaying side to side in waves, then turning into ribbons that endlessly fold and flip, splitting into countless finer, paler streamers, until, divided smaller and smaller, they vanish. Sometimes it makes you suspect the smoke hasn't disappeared at all, only that the limits of human sight can't catch it. Now and then a few little ribbons recombine and drift a bit farther off, and all the while I kept hoping a few strands of blue would turn into a drifting, shifting cloud and linger long in the room. So I did my utmost to keep my hand still — and though my arm went numb, there wasn't much change: they all broke apart and dissolved too.
